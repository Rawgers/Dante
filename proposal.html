Dante: Trial by Fire

Michael Hsiu, Roger Luo, Christopher Qian

Milestone

Final

Summary:

In this project, we first attempt to simulate wind using wind field models. We then apply this wind field to a fire simulation.

Abstract:

Wind is an interesting phenomenon because it affects the behavior of the world without being visible. These properties of wind make it challenging to be simulated in computer graphics. Nonetheless, convincing, efficient simulations of wind are important in creating realistic representations of the world. In this project, we adopt the work of Dr. Khorloo Oyundolgor in A Study on Efficient Algorithms for Generating Virtual Wind Field Usable for Real-time Animation. Using Oyundolgor’s noise-based algorithm, we generate wind field maps in real time to demonstrate the computational efficiency of simulating wind. We proceed to apply this wind field to a fire animation simulation to demonstrate a manifestation of the wind field. Along the way, we also explore the similarities between simulating wind and fire.

Goals and Deliverables:

Our first deliverable will be snapshots of a wind field, a color-coded vector field of wind direction and turbulency, generated in real time. Our second and final deliverable will be an application of this wind field in a fire simulation with customizable parameters that affect the appearance and animation of the simulation. Should we have more time, we also hope to simulate fire spread, different kinds of fire—such as a candle flame—and a simulation of wind on a field of grass using 2D wind field over grass generated by Bézier curves.

In order to accomplish this we should have a fully functioning and parameterized model for simulating wind, and have this wind interact with the fire in a natural way. This should all be done efficiently in real time. If everything goes well, more work can be done to make the fire more realistic, like applying textures to the particles and making the fire grow/shrink in response and over time.

We will gauge performance by whether or not the simulation can be rendered on a laptop with an Intel 2.5GHz CPU processor and AMD Radeon R9 M370X graphics card at 30fps. For accuracy, we will reference the wind field output and fire simulation images shown in the Oyundolgor paper and compare the results. Lastly, we will try to subjectively evaluate how natural and convincing the fire looks.

With our analysis we hope to answer the questions of how realistic the generated wind and fire look and how efficient they are to compute and render. Some other questions we hope to answer are what parameters to use for noise generation to best simulate realistic wind, what what parameters for wind speed we can use the generate the best and most realistic looking effects on the fire.

Schedule:

Our schedule will be divided into three main parts: simulating the wind, simulating the fire, and preparing materials for the final paper and presentation. For the first part the main tasks involve creating the wind field, following the 7 step model proposed in the Oyundolgor paper. This is predicted to be the most challenging part of the project. The wind simulation will first be static with a constant wind field and then made dynamic using random noise as outlined in the paper, simulating changing wind velocities. Deliverables visualizing the wind field will have to be made.

The second part involves creating the fire and having the wind act upon the fire. For the fire we plan to use Unity’s particles generator which allows individual control over their particles. Each of the particles will be acted upon by the wind velocities in the wind field. Additional work may be done to make the fire more realistic looking, such as through applying textures.

The final part involves creating the writeup and presentation deliverables. We will create an interactive demo with a fire blowing in the wind for the main presentation. This demo will allow customizing wind speeds and directions in real time. We will also do a writeup of the techniques presented in the paper along with our own visualizations of the wind field and fire.

Resources:

Our main resource will be the paper A Study on Efficient Algorithms for Generating Virtual Wind Field Usable for Real-Time Animation (2012) by Khorloo Oyundolgor. This paper outlines simulating wind and its applications to different domains, including grass, cloth, and fire simulation. We will use Unity as our rendering engine.
